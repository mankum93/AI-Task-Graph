# Pilot Sample

This page explains what the `PILOT_SAMPLE` node actually does in this example.

## Purpose of the pilot chapter

The pilot chapter is not a toy.

Its job is to reveal whether the graph has the right quality shape for the rest of the book.

The chapter should be representative enough to test:
- explanatory depth
- tone and teaching rhythm
- standards adherence
- examples
- diagram usefulness
- section transitions
- code / visual / prose coupling

## Why the pilot is mandatory here

This task is highly subjective.

The project already has standards, but that does **not** guarantee safe scale-out. The framework assumes that long writing projects often degrade after a strong start, so the graph must verify quality before allowing more chapter work.

## Recommended pilot chapter choice

The pilot should not be the easiest chapter.

A stronger choice is a chapter that forces the system to demonstrate:
- teaching clarity
- technical correctness
- diagram usefulness
- section progression
- adherence to chapter contracts

In other words, choose a chapter that exposes the real quality bar.

## What the pilot node produces

A strong pilot node should output:
- one full representative chapter draft
- section structure aligned to the chapter spec
- code and diagram planning where appropriate
- explicit notes on which standards governed the work
- any constraints or ambiguities discovered during execution

## Internal self-checks inside the node

Even before external QA nodes run, the pilot node should check:
- did it actually follow the standards stack?
- is the chapter over-regular or templated?
- did it fall into shortcut language?
- are visuals integrated into teaching or just mentioned?
- is the chapter thinner than the standards imply?

## What success looks like

The pilot chapter is successful when it shows:
- strong standards adherence
- strong explanatory energy
- real reader usefulness
- no obvious early templatization
- a shape that seems repeatable without immediate collapse

## What failure looks like

The pilot fails if:
- the chapter is technically fine but dead or generic
- it already sounds templated
- it feels shorter, thinner, or safer than the standards call for
- diagrams are weak or detached from the prose
- the chapter obeys headings but misses the real teaching purpose

If the pilot fails, the graph should **not** scale.

## Why this matters

Without a pilot node, a book workflow often confuses “we can generate chapters” with “we can generate good chapters repeatedly.”

The pilot exists to make that distinction visible before the system spends large amounts of effort on the wrong execution pattern.
